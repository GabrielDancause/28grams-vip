The Main Types of Yeast

Yeast is a living microorganism that feeds on sugars in flour, releasing carbon dioxide gas that makes dough rise. Knowing the difference between the main types is essential for substituting them effectively.

  • Instant Yeast: The most robust and concentrated form of commercial yeast. It can be mixed directly into dry ingredients without blooming. Also known as "Rapid Rise" or "Bread Machine Yeast."
  • Active Dry Yeast: Contains larger granules and dormant yeast cells that usually require dissolving (blooming) in warm water before use to ensure they are active. It is about 25% less potent by weight than instant yeast.
  • Fresh Yeast: Also known as cake yeast or compressed yeast. It is highly perishable, sold in solid blocks, and contains mostly water. It is very active but requires using about 3x the weight of instant yeast.
  • Sourdough Starter: A wild yeast culture maintained in a mixture of flour and water. It ferments much slower than commercial yeast and adds a distinct tangy flavor to baked goods.

How to Convert Yeast Types

To substitute one yeast for another, you can generally follow these weight ratios. Our calculator uses these exact standardized baking industry conversions:

  • 100% Instant Yeast = Base multiplier (1.0x)
  • 125% Active Dry Yeast = 1.25x Instant Yeast
  • 300% Fresh Yeast = 3.00x Instant Yeast
  • ~1400% Sourdough Starter (100% hydration) = Generally, you can substitute 7g of instant yeast with roughly 100g of active sourdough starter. However, since starter is 50% flour and 50% water, you must subtract 50g of flour and 50g of water from your original recipe.

Temperature and Fermentation Time

Yeast activity is highly dependent on temperature. The "Rule of 17" in baking states that for every 1°C increase in dough temperature, the fermentation time decreases by approximately 10%. Alternatively, a general rule of thumb is that fermentation time halves for every 8°C (15°F) increase in ambient temperature.

Our temperature adjuster uses a standard exponential decay model based on these principles to help you estimate how long your dough will take to rise if your kitchen is warmer or cooler than the recipe specifies.
